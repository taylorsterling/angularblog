Stream Life


So Much Tech

So if you’ve ever considered streaming before, you likely did some research and noticed how much technology is involved. Depending on what gaming platform you use (if you stream for gaming) you need a capture card, you need some kind of streaming software, a decent microphone, webcam, etc. It’s intimidating at first, and can deter a lot of would- be streamers from ever getting into the swing of it. The bills rack up pretty quickly for streaming equipment if you go all- out, and that can cause a headache too.



I started somewhat simplistically and streamed straight from my PC. It was easy enough to just download Streamlabs OBS and use window capture to stream Overwatch gameplay. I started out playing with a friend, but as we created social media accounts and dove deeper into the gaming community, we both quickly started making a lot of friends. Before we knew it, we had multi- streams happening with groups of other streamers/ gamers!



Going With the Flow

Streaming isn’t like any other form of entertainment. I’ve performed in musicals and plays, band concerts and dance recitals, but livestreaming digitally for an audience you can’t see is wildly different. I personally stream using a webcam so my audience can see me. Although plenty of people stream without, I prefer being able to watch a broadcaster’s expressions as they laugh or rage. It makes the experience of watching a stream more entertaining for me, so that’s what I try to do for my own viewers.



While streaming, it looks like all fun and games- and it is- just with some added techniques. For example, yes of course the overall objective is to have a good time and play a game while others can enjoy it with you (if you do gaming streams). There is, however, a balance between relaxing while just playing a game, and interacting with your audience. I love as big an audience as I can get, so for me it’s absolutely imperative that I try my best to respond to chat messages and keep talking throughout the stream. I'm pretty adamant that my viewers never listen to silence during gameplay. Everyone has their own stream style though, and every viewer has preferences on commentary, but I love to talk and joke, so I try to keep my streams bubbly and humorous.



From Start to Finish

My stream is set up to have an intro before I go on so that my followers get the update that I’m live and have a little time to hop on Twitch before I actually begin. Once I start, it’s easy to forget to check chat, but thankfully one of my mods- and even my husband- will text me and remind me to respond.


When I’m playing a game, I always try to remember how my emotions and actions will affect my audience. For example, there was a jump in the first game of the Spyro Reignited Trilogy that I just could not get past. Before I react, I need to remember that people are watching me. If I get frustrated and rage, are my viewers going to be turned off, or will they think it’s funny? Over time it starts to come naturally as you figure out what sorts of reactions to events in a game your specific audience will respond best to.


It can be a little nerve- racking at first to get into streaming, but once you get going it's so much fun. If you really commit to it, it can become a good way to make some extra cash, too! Stay tuned for more articles on how to become and remain a Twitch affiliate.
